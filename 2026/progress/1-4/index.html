Inline elements

HTML is the language that gives web pages their basic structure. It tells a browser what each piece of content is supposed to be, such as a title, a section heading, a paragraph of text, a list of items, a link to another page, or an image description. You can think of it as the blueprint for a webpage: it doesn’t make things look fancy on its own, but it makes sure everything is organized and understandable. That’s why HTML is usually the first thing people learn when they want to create websites.
The core idea behind HTML is marking up content so the browser can display it correctly. You write your content and label parts of it as different kinds of elements, like headings and paragraphs, and the browser follows those labels to lay out the page.
Most elements have a clear beginning and end, and they can be placed inside one another to form a neat structure, like sections containing headings and text. A typical webpage includes information the visitor sees, plus some behind-the-scenes details that help the browser understand the page, such as the page’s title and other helpful metadata.
